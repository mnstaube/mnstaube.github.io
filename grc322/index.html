Bodoni vs. Didot

History of each typeface

Didot

Didot has been a typeface being used and implemented into the print and later digital industry for over 100 years. The typeface was created by Firmin Didot who was heavily immersed in the print world during the early 1800s. The Didot family built a dynasty of French printers, which inspired Firmin Didot to produce a typeface from his expertise in the print world. The typeface is often named the first Modern typeface. Didot has been used for the CBS News, and the logotype for The Late Show with Stephen Colbert. This typeface has been often associated with luxury, leading to many fashion publications to use this typeface. Today, Linotype Didot offers a variety of twelve different weights, expanding from its original origins in the early 1800s in France. Currently, Vogue utilizes Didot’s typeface, proving its long lasting legacy. There are a few different typefaces that this family produced but the most famous of these were designed between 1784-1811 in Paris. Firmin Didot cut and cast the letters and Pierre Didot would print with those type pieces. This typeface was designed with increased stroke contrast, condensed armature, hairline strokes, vertical stress, and flat, unbracketed serifs. It is considered a neoclassical serif typeface. There are a lot of different variations of Didot and there is not one that can be considered as the original but Firmin spent 27 years experimenting with the design.

Bodoni

Bodoni is a Modern serif typeface created in 1798 that was adapted and used in publications during the late 1700s. Gianbasttista Bodoni was inspired by typefaces such as Didot. The main characteristics of Bodoni are the extreme contrasting fine serfs and the serifs are 90 degrees. Bodoni drew inspiration from Baskerville. What gave Bodoni its signature look was its “Q” and “J” characters which soon gained popularity during the 1700s. At the time of establishment, Bodoni was cast from metal despite the typeface’s intricate characteristics for the time’s standards. Some popular uses of Bodoni today are the Mama Mia posters which adopted Bodoni’s modern typeface for both film and theater productions. Additionally, popular brands like Giorgio Armani and Calvin Klein select Bodoni as their typeface to help bring to life their brands and communicate to consumers their identities. When the typeface initially was released and popularized within the publishing and print industry, there was a large amount of success and was favored by many due to its intricate, high quality design. However, there were several complaints that there were legibility issues but based on its intended purpose, it achieved its impressively intricate design and was not necessarily aiming to be implemented as a practical typeface.

Comparison

There are subtle, yet noticeable differences between these two typefaces. One main difference is that the hairline strokes for Didot are thinner than those of Bodoni. Furthermore, the counters of Didot have more space, whereas the counters are narrower in Bodoni. The x-height of Bodoni is shorter than that of Didot. In Bodoni there is a use of a ball terminal whereas the terminal for Didot is a teardrop shape. The overall strokes of Bodoni are also thicker in comparison to Didot.

Similarities

As both typefaces are Modern, they share many similarities, such as strong contrast between thick and thin stroke, hairline serifs, and vertical stress.
